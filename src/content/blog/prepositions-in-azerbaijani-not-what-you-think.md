In English, prepositions come before the word they modify.

*In the house. For you. With a friend. About the language.*

The prefix "pre" in preposition literally means before. The word sits in front.

Azerbaijani does the opposite.

The equivalent words come after the noun, not before it. That makes them postpositions. And they do not work alone; they pair with specific case suffixes on the noun. Once you understand which postposition pairs with which case, the system becomes predictable.


## What case suffixes already handle

Before getting to postpositions, it is worth noting that Azerbaijani case suffixes already carry a lot of the work that English prepositions do.

The dative suffix **-a / -ə** covers "to" and "toward."
The locative **-da / -də** covers "in," "at," and "on."
The ablative **-dan / -dən** covers "from" and "out of."

So **evdə** means *in the house*, **evə** means *to the house*, and **evdən** means *from the house*, all without a separate word. The suffix does it.

If case suffixes are still new, [Understanding Azerbaijani Cases: A Practical Approach](/blog/understanding-azerbaijani-cases-practical-approach/) covers the full system.

Postpositions handle the relationships that suffixes alone cannot express.


## Postpositions that pair with the dative (-a / -ə)

These postpositions follow a noun that takes the dative suffix.

**tərəf / sarı** - toward / in the direction of

> **Evə tərəf.** - *Toward the house.*  
> **Şəhərə sarı getdik.** - *We went in the direction of the city.*


**doğru** - toward / straight to

> **Meşəyə doğru.** - *Toward the forest.*  
> **Həqiqətə doğru.** - *Toward the truth.*


**qarşı** - against / opposite / toward

> **Sənə qarşı.** - *Against you.*  
> **Evə qarşı durdu.** - *He stood opposite the house.*


**dair / aid** - regarding / about / relative to

> **Bu işə dair.** - *Regarding this matter.*  
> **Dilə aid suallar.** - *Questions related to language.*


**görə** - according to / because of / for

> **Sənə görə.** - *Because of you. / For you.*  
> **Qaydaya görə.** - *According to the rule.*


**qədər** - until / as much as / up to

> **Axşama qədər.** - *Until evening.*  
> **Sənə qədər gəldim.** - *I came as far as you.*


## Postpositions that pair with the ablative (-dan / -dən)

These follow a noun in the ablative case.

**başqa / savayı / qeyri** - except / besides / apart from

> **Səndən başqa.** - *Except you.*  
> **Bundan savayı.** - *Besides this.*


**bəri** - since

> **Səhərdən bəri.** - *Since morning.*  
> **Uşaqlıqdan bəri.** - *Since childhood.*


**ötrü** - for / for the sake of (similar to üçün, slightly more poetic)

> **Səndən ötrü.** - *For your sake.*


**yana** - regarding / on the part of

> **Uşaqdan yana.** - *Regarding the child.*  
> **Ondan yana danışdı.** - *He spoke on behalf of him.*


**sonra** - after

> **İşdən sonra.** - *After work.*  
> **Yeməkdən sonra.** - *After the meal.*


**əvvəl** - before

> **Səhər yeməyindən əvvəl.** - *Before breakfast.*


**başlayaraq** - starting from

> **Bu gündən başlayaraq.** - *Starting from today.*

## Postpositions with the genitive and nominative both


**üçün** - for / for the sake of

> **Sənin üçün.** - *For you.*  (genitive)

> **Bu iş üçün vaxt lazımdır.** - *Time is needed for this work.* (nominative)



**haqqında** - about

> **Sənin haqqında düşünürəm.** - *I am thinking about you.* (genitive)

> **Dil haqqında.** - *About language.*  (nominative


## Postpositions with the nominative

Some postpositions follow a noun with no case suffix at all.

**kimi** - like / as

> **Uşaq kimi danışır.** - *He speaks like a child.*


**tək** - like / as (more poetic or casual than kimi)

> **Gül tək.** - *Like a flower.*  
> **Aslan tək güclüdür.** - *He is strong like a lion.*


**ilə** - with / by means of

> **Dost ilə.** - *With a friend.*  
> **Qatar ilə.** - *By train.*


## ilə in spoken Azerbaijani

**ilə** is worth its own note because it behaves differently in real speech.

In written Azerbaijani, **ilə** stands as a separate word. In spoken Azerbaijani, it almost always shrinks into a suffix attached directly to the noun: **-la** after back vowels, **-lə** after front vowels.

> **Qatar ilə** → **Qatarla** - *By train*  
> **Dost ilə** → **Dostla** - *With a friend*  
> **Həvəs ilə** → **Həvəslə** - *With enthusiasm*

If you have been wondering why things sometimes sound compressed in natural speech, this is one of the clearest examples. The written and spoken forms are genuinely different. [Why Everything in Azerbaijani Sounds Too Fast](/blog/why-everything-in-azerbaijani-sounds-too-fast) goes into this pattern more broadly.


## The logic behind it

The system is more consistent than it looks at first.

Dative postpositions generally deal with direction, purpose, or relationship toward something. Ablative postpositions generally deal with origin, separation, or reference point. Once you internalize which case carries which kind of meaning, you start to predict which postposition will pair with which suffix.

It is not memorization. It is pattern recognition.

And that is the same thing that makes Azerbaijani grammar rewarding once it starts to click: the rules are consistent, the suffixes are logical, and the more you see the system in action, the more predictable it becomes.
