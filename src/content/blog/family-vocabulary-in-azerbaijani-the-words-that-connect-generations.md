English has one word for aunt. One word for uncle. One word for cousin.

Azerbaijani has four words for aunt and uncle alone, one for each combination of parent's side and gender. Cousins are identified by which relative connects you. The language encodes which side of the family someone belongs to, and that distinction matters socially, not just linguistically.

This is one of the places where Azerbaijani is genuinely richer than English. Learning these terms is not just vocabulary. It is learning how Azerbaijani culture organizes family, loyalty, and belonging.


## The immediate family

**ana** - mother  
**ata** - father  
**qardaş** - brother  
**bacı** - sister  
**oğul** - son  
**qız** - daughter  

Unlike English, Azerbaijani distinguishes younger siblings in everyday speech.

**balaca qardaş** - younger brother  
**balaca bacı** - younger sister  

This is not just descriptive. How you relate to a sibling, how you address them, how much deference is expected, all of this shifts depending on age.

Spouses:

**ər** - husband  
**arvad** - wife  


## Grandparents

**nənə** - grandmother  
**baba** - grandfather  

These are the terms used across both sides of the family. Simple, consistent, and among the first words most Azerbaijani children say.


## Aunts and uncles: four distinct words

English has aunt and uncle. Azerbaijani has four terms, one for each combination of parent's side and gender.

**xala** - mother's sister  
**əmi** - father's brother  
**bibi** - father's sister  
**dayı** - mother's brother  

These are not interchangeable. Calling your father's sister **xala** instead of **bibi** is a real error, not just a stylistic one. The terms carry different social weight and different implied relationships.

**Dayı** in particular carries warmth in Azerbaijani culture. A mother's brother is traditionally seen as a close and protective figure, and the word itself is sometimes used affectionately beyond the literal family relationship.


## Cousins: children of which aunt or uncle?

English has one word: cousin.

Azerbaijani specifies whose child they are.

**xala uşağı** - child of your xala (mother's sister's child)  
**əmi uşağı** - child of your əmi (father's brother's child)  
**bibi uşağı** - child of your bibi (father's sister's child)  
**dayı uşağı** - child of your dayı (mother's brother's child)  

When gender matters, **uşağı** (child) is replaced with **oğlu** (son) or **qızı** (daughter):

**xala oğlu** - son of your mother's sister  
**xala qızı** - daughter of your mother's sister  
**dayı oğlu** - son of your mother's brother  
**əmi qızı** - daughter of your father's brother  

The cousin's identity is defined by which relative connects you, and if needed, by their gender too.


## In-laws

**qaynata** - father-in-law (spouse's father)  
**qaynanа** - mother-in-law (spouse's mother)  
**qayın** - brother-in-law (spouse's brother)  
**baldız** - sister-in-law (spouse's sister)  
**kürəkən** - son-in-law  
**gəlin** - daughter-in-law / bride  

**Gəlin** deserves special mention. It means both daughter-in-law and bride, and it carries significant cultural weight. A **gəlin** entering a family is a major social event, and the word itself signals a shift in relationships and responsibilities. You will hear it often at weddings and in family conversations.


## Terms of address vs. terms of reference

There is a distinction worth knowing.

When Azerbaijanis talk about a family member, they use the terms above. But when they talk directly to a family member, they often use the term itself as a form of address, the same way English speakers might say "Mom" or "Dad."

> **Ana, gəl bura.** - *Mom, come here.*  
> **Baba, sən haradasan?** - *Grandpa, where are you?*  
> **Dayı, sağ ol.** - *Uncle, thank you.*  

This also extends beyond the family. Azerbaijanis often use family terms to address strangers warmly, especially older people. Calling an older woman **xala** or an older man **əmi** on the street is a sign of respect, not familiarity. It places them in a social role that the culture recognizes.


## Why these words matter for learners

If you are a heritage learner, these terms are probably already in your ears, even if you never consciously learned them. They are the words that show up at family gatherings, in phone calls with relatives, in the stories older family members tell.

If you are learning Azerbaijani from outside the culture, these terms are a window into how Azerbaijani society is organized. Family in Azerbaijan is not just immediate household. It is an extended network with specific roles, specific relationships, and specific language for all of it.

Knowing who is a **xala** and who is a **bibi** is not a grammar exercise. It is knowing how to show up correctly in an Azerbaijani family context.
